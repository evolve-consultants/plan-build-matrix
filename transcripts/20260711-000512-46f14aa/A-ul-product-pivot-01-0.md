Good place to start. Here's a practical way to think through it:

**First, get clear on why this tool exists**

- What specific problem does it solve, and how painful is that problem for people outside your consultancy?
- Is it valuable because it's genuinely well-built, or because it's paired with your team's expertise/services? A lot of "internal tools" are really just thin interfaces on top of institutional knowledge — without you in the loop, they don't do much.

**Then validate demand before building anything new**

- Talk to 10-15 people who aren't your existing clients but have the problem you solve. Don't pitch — ask how they currently handle it, what they've tried, what they'd pay for.
- Look for signal that people have *actively sought solutions* (bought something, hired someone, built a janky spreadsheet) — not just "yeah that'd be nice to have."
- If you can, get a few of them to use the tool as-is, ugly warts and all, before you invest in productizing.

**Be honest about the gap between "internal tool" and "product"**

Internal tools usually assume a lot of context: your team configures it, fixes it when it breaks, interprets the output. A product needs:
- Onboarding that doesn't require you
- Error handling for cases your team would just work around
- Documentation
- Some kind of support/success motion
- Pricing and packaging

This gap is usually much bigger than founders expect. It's often 70% of the real work.

**Figure out the business model tension early**

Consultancies and products have different economics and incentives — services are high-touch/custom/billable-hours, products are scalable/standardized/low-touch. Ask yourself:
- Are you trying to build a separate product business, or a lead-gen/retention tool for the consultancy?
- Who owns this internally? A product needs a dedicated owner, not "whoever has spare time between client work."

**A concrete next step**

Pick the single workflow your tool handles that you're most confident is valuable to outsiders. Find 5 non-clients with that exact problem. Give them access for free in exchange for feedback. See if they actually use it unprompted after week one. That'll tell you more than any amount of planning.

What does the tool actually do, and who's it built for right now?
