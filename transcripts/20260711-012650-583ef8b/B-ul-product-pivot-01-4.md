### The Decision Tree

**Start here: Is there external demand?**

This is the first filter. If no one outside your firm would pay for this, productizing fails before it starts.

| | Method | Output | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Talk to customers first** | 5–10 structured interviews with people in your market who face the same problem | Demand signals + feature priorities | 2–3 weeks |
| **B. Survey + landing page** | Quick online survey (your audience) + simple landing page with email signup | Volume + intent signal | 1–2 weeks |
| **C. Freemium pilot** | Offer the tool free/cheap to a cohort of non-competitors; track adoption and feedback | Real behavior + retention data | 4–8 weeks |

**Why A is usually the best first move:**
You want to hear people describe their problem in their words before you decide what to build or how to price it. Interviews also let you identify non-obvious customer segments. A landing page assumes you already know the message; surveys assume people will be honest when asked directly.

### If demand looks real, then assess build feasibility

**Questions to answer:**
- Can you extract these tools from your internal environment without gutting your own operations?
- Are they documented and generalizable, or would each customer need custom integration?
- Do you have the maintainability bandwidth if you're running a consultancy *and* supporting a product?
- Are there legal/IP complications (client data, contracts, third-party dependencies)?

### Recommendation
Start with **Option A: structured customer interviews** (5–10 people). Don't build anything yet. Don't even decide if you're serious yet.

Why: You're testing a hypothesis, not validating a feature list. Interviews force you to listen rather than sell. They also let you gauge whether demand is broad enough to support a product, or whether you'd just be building a faster way to serve the same five types of clients you already have.
