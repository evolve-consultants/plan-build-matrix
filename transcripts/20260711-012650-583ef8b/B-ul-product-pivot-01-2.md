### The key distinction
There are really two separate questions here:
1. **Should I productize?** (validation question)
2. **How do I productize?** (execution question)

Most people skip #1 and regret it. You can spend months building a product for a market that doesn't exist, or for customers who'd never pay what it costs to build.

### Three paths forward

| | Upside | Downside | Best when |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A: Validate first, then build** | You de-risk before investing heavily. You may discover a much bigger market than you thought, or find out demand doesn't justify the effort. Either outcome saves you time. | Takes 4-8 weeks of real conversations before you even know if it's worth pursuing. Slower to "product." | You're cautious, have limited spare capacity, or the tool is still rough. This is the safer, slower path. |
| **B: Build a paid pilot** | You learn what customers actually want while they're paying. You generate early revenue and validate demand simultaneously. Faster to traction. | You might build the wrong thing at first, or commit to features that don't scale. Riskier if the market isn't there. | You have capacity, the tool is pretty solid, and you're confident enough in the problem to take on some execution risk. |
| **C: Open-source + slow monetization** | You build credibility, get feedback at scale, and find your true customers organically. Low risk. | Very slow to revenue. Monetization is hard to retrofit. Best if you have patience and the tool solves a problem for a large community. | The tool solves a problem that affects a lot of people who aren't currently paying for solutions. |

### Recommendation
**Start with a lightweight validation (Path A), which takes 4–6 weeks.**

Here's why: You already have a time-tested tool and deep domain expertise. That's valuable. But consultancies often build tools that solve *their specific workflow* — not necessarily problems other people have the same way. A few conversations with people outside your company will either confirm you're onto something or save you months of build time.

This is fast: 15–20 structured customer conversations, not a market research deck. Talk to:
- Non-customers in your industry who might use this (peers, adjacent firms)
- People who tried to solve the same problem another way
- People who decided not to solve it (why?)

The output is simple: do you hear a consistent problem statement and willingness to pay? If yes, move to Path B. If no, you learned something valuable for cheaper than building.
