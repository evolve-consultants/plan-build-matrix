Here's a practical sequence:

**First, be honest about what you actually have:**
- What tool specifically? (Is it genuinely novel, or a clever wrapper around existing solutions?)
- How much of its value comes from your domain expertise vs. the tool itself?
- Would others pay for it standalone, or did *your* context make it valuable?

**Then test that assumption cheaply:**
- Talk to 10-15 people in your target market (not friends). Ask if they'd use it, what they'd pay.
- Offer it to 2-3 friendly companies at a low price. Watch how they actually use it.
- If they don't adopt it quickly or need heavy customization, that's data.

**Only *then* invest in productizing:**
- Document what it actually does (you'll discover gaps)
- Strip out internal-specific logic
- Build a bare-bones version others can self-serve
- Figure out pricing/packaging

**The hard part nobody mentions:**
- Supporting users is completely different from using it internally
- You'll discover your "simple tool" had lots of hidden assumptions
- Sales/marketing effort often exceeds development effort

**Common trap:** Assuming because it solved your problem, others will buy it. Most internal tools are solving a very specific configuration of your business.

What's the tool, roughly? That changes whether this is worth exploring.
