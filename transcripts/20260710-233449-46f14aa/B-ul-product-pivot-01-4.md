### Why these matter

The entry point changes completely based on answers. If you're sitting on a competitive advantage that 50 firms in your space would pay for, you're path A. If you've got a nice-to-have that might interest *some* people, you're path B. If the main value is that it works for *your specific process*, you might not have a product at all — you might have a service differentiator instead.

### Rough decision tree

**Start here** (before any product work):
1. **Validate demand** — Talk to 5–10 non-customers in your space. Describe the problem it solves (not the tool). Do they recognize it? Do they pay for a solution today?
2. **Map the competition** — Google, ProductHunt, G2. What exists? How are you different? (If the answer is "it's customized to us," that's a sign it's not a product yet.)
3. **Clarify your model** — Are you selling to consultancies? SMBs? Enterprises? Is it SaaS, one-time license, or installed on-premise?

**If demand looks real**, the next phase splits:
- **Lower-friction path**: package it minimally, charge for it, see if anyone buys. (6–8 weeks, small team, learn fast)
- **Higher-polish path**: invest in UI, onboarding, docs, support infrastructure. (4–6 months, bigger commitment, aim for product-market fit before launch)
