### The Real First Decision Tree

Rather than options, here's the actual sequence—because the first move determines everything downstream:

**1. Validate demand (before building anything)**
- Are your clients asking for this, or do you think they should want it?
  - *If asking*: You have signal. Move to #2.
  - *If not asking*: You need to test the hypothesis. Do 5–10 customer interviews with non-clients in your target market (not your existing clients—they're biased toward your solutions). Ask if they have this problem and what they'd pay. If fewer than 3 would seriously consider it, this stays internal.

**2. Define the product boundary (not the same as the tool)**
- Your internal tool solves *your* problem. The product solves a *market* problem.
- What specific job does it do for a paying customer? (Not "project management tool"—*"enables freelancers to forecast capacity without spreadsheets"*)
- Who is the customer? (Not "consultants"—*"service-based SMBs with 5–50 people"*)
- This clarity determines everything: pricing, positioning, go-to-market, feature set, support model.

**3. Assess the rework burden (honestly)**
- Internal tools are built for you—your workflows, your tolerances, your context.
- Turning it into a product means: documentation, error handling for edge cases you never hit, onboarding, support, security/compliance, backwards compatibility, probably a UI refresh.
- Estimate: Is this 2 weeks of work or 6 months? The answer changes whether this is a nights-and-weekends play or a real business decision.

**4. Choose a go-to-market hypothesis**
- Do you sell it to consultancies like yours (you know the buyer, the problem, the sales motion)?
- Do you go horizontal (broader market, bigger TAM, harder to reach)?
- Do you keep it behind your consultancy as a service differentiator (no separate product, just a competitive moat)?
- This choice is almost as important as the product itself.

### What You Should Do Next

Before committing to anything, spend **two weeks** on validation:

1. **Schedule 5 customer interviews** with people in your target market who *aren't* your clients. Ask:
   - Do they have this problem? How bad is it?
   - How do they currently solve it?
   - Would they consider buying a tool for this?
   - Ballpark: what would they pay?

2. **Write a one-page product positioning statement**:
   - For [target buyer], [product name] solves [specific job] by [how]. Unlike [alternatives], it [key differentiator].
   - Be specific. If you can't write this clearly, you don't understand the market yet.

3. **Document the rework estimate**:
   - List what would have to change for a stranger to use this tool.
   - Break it into buckets: critical path, nice-to-have, research questions.
   - Time-box it.
